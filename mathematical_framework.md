## 1. Foundational Definitions

### 1.1 Geometric Algebra Cl(d, 0)

Let $\mathbb{R}^d$ be a $d$-dimensional Euclidean vector space with orthonormal basis $\{e_1, e_2, \ldots, e_d\}$ satisfying:

$$e_i \cdot e_j = \delta_{ij} \quad \text{and} \quad e_i \wedge e_j = -e_j \wedge e_i$$

The **geometric algebra** $\text{Cl}(d, 0)$ is the associative algebra generated by $\mathbb{R}^d$ under the **geometric product**:

$$e_i e_j = e_i \cdot e_j + e_i \wedge e_j = \delta_{ij} + e_i \wedge e_j$$

From this, we derive the fundamental identities:

- **Square of a basis vector**: $e_i^2 = 1$ for all $i$
- **Anticommutation**: $e_i e_j = -e_j e_i$ for $i \neq j$
- **Associativity**: $(AB)C = A(BC)$ for all $A, B, C \in \text{Cl}(d, 0)$

### 1.2 The Multivector

An arbitrary multivector $A \in \text{Cl}(d, 0)$ decomposes uniquely into grades:

$$A = \sum_{k=0}^{d} \langle A \rangle_k = \langle A \rangle_0 + \langle A \rangle_1 + \cdots + \langle A \rangle_d$$

where $\langle A \rangle_k$ is the **grade-$k$ part** of $A$:
- $\langle A \rangle_0 = a_0 \in \mathbb{R}$ (scalar)
- $\langle A \rangle_1 = \sum_{i=1}^{d} a_i e_i$ (vector)
- $\langle A \rangle_2 = \sum_{1 \leq i < j \leq d} a_{ij} e_i \wedge e_j$ (bivector)
- $\langle A \rangle_k = \sum_{1 \leq i_1 < \cdots < i_k \leq d} a_{i_1 \ldots i_k} e_{i_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge e_{i_k}$ (k-vector)
- $\langle A \rangle_d = a_{12\ldots d} e_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge e_d$ (pseudoscalar)

**Dimension**: $\dim \text{Cl}(d, 0) = 2^d$. For $d = 4$: $\dim = 16$.

The basis blades are indexed by subsets $I \subseteq \{1, \ldots, d\}$:

$$e_I = e_{i_1} e_{i_2} \cdots e_{i_k} \quad \text{where } I = \{i_1 < i_2 < \cdots < i_k\}$$

The total number of basis blades of grade $k$ is $\binom{d}{k}$.

### 1.3 The Geometric Product

For arbitrary multivectors $A, B \in \text{Cl}(d, 0)$, the geometric product $AB$ is defined by bilinearity and the basis identity $e_i e_j = \delta_{ij} + e_i \wedge e_j$.

**Theorem 1 (Grade Structure of Geometric Product).** For homogeneous multivectors (blades) $A_r \in \text{Cl}^r(d,0)$ and $B_s \in \text{Cl}^s(d,0)$ of grades $r$ and $s$:

$$A_r B_s = \sum_{k=0}^{\min(r,s)} \langle A_r B_s \rangle_{s-r+2k}$$

The geometric product produces grades ranging from $|r-s|$ to $r+s$ in steps of 2.

**Corollary 1.1 (Specific Grade Products):**

| Product | Grades Produced |
|---------|----------------|
| Scalar (0) × Scalar (0) | 0 |
| Scalar (0) × Vector (1) | 1 |
| Scalar (0) × Bivector (2) | 2 |
| Vector (1) × Vector (1) | 0, 2 |
| Vector (1) × Bivector (2) | 1, 3 |
| Vector (1) × Trivector (3) | 2, 4 |
| Bivector (2) × Bivector (2) | 0, 2, 4 |
| Bivector (2) × Trivector (3) | 1, 3 |
| Trivector (3) × Trivector (3) | 0, 2, 4 |
| Bivector (2) × 4-vector (4) | 2 |

**Proof.** Follows from the fundamental identity and grade-by-grade expansion. For bivector × bivector: if $A = e_{ij}$ and $B = e_{kl}$, then $AB = e_{ij}e_{kl}$. If $\{i,j\} \cap \{k,l\} = \emptyset$, then $AB = e_{ijkl}$ (grade 4). If they share one index, e.g., $e_{12}e_{13} = -e_1 e_2 e_1 e_3 = e_2 e_3 = e_{23}$ (grade 2). If they share both, $e_{12}e_{12} = -e_1 e_2 e_1 e_2 = e_1^2 e_2^2 = 1$ (grade 0). $\square$

### 1.4 Key Operators and Involutions

**Definition 1 (Grade Projection).** The grade-$k$ projection operator:

$$\langle A \rangle_k = \frac{1}{2^d} \sum_{B \in \mathcal{B}} \langle B^{-1} A B \rangle_k$$

where $\mathcal{B}$ is the set of basis blades of grade $k$.

**Definition 2 (Reverse/Reversion).** The reversion $\tilde{A}$ reverses the order of all basis blades:

$$\widetilde{e_{i_1} e_{i_2} \cdots e_{i_k}} = e_{i_k} \cdots e_{i_2} e_{i_1}$$

For a grade-$k$ blade: $\tilde{A}_k = (-1)^{k(k-1)/2} A_k$. Thus:
- Scalars and vectors are self-reversing
- Bivectors reverse sign: $\widetilde{e_{ij}} = -e_{ij}$
- Trivectors are self-reversing
- The pseudoscalar in Cl(4,0) reverses sign

**Definition 3 (Clifford Conjugation).** The Clifford conjugate $\overline{A}$ combines reversion and grade involution:

$$\overline{A} = \widehat{\tilde{A}}$$

where the grade involution $\widehat{A} = \sum_k (-1)^k \langle A \rangle_k$.

**Definition 4 (Norm).** The norm of a multivector:

$$|A|^2 = \langle A \tilde{A} \rangle_0 = \langle \tilde{A} A \rangle_0$$

For a blade $A_k$, $|A_k|^2 = \langle A_k \tilde{A}_k \rangle_0 = (-1)^{k(k-1)/2} \langle A_k^2 \rangle_0$.

**Definition 5 (Inverse).** A multivector $A$ is invertible if $|A|^2 \neq 0$:

$$A^{-1} = \frac{\tilde{A}}{|A|^2}$$

**Definition 6 (Pseudoscalar and Duality).** The unit pseudoscalar $I = e_1 e_2 \cdots e_d$ satisfies $I^2 = (-1)^{d(d-1)/2}$. The Hodge dual of $A$ is:

$$A^* = A I^{-1} = A \tilde{I}$$

In Cl(4,0): $I^2 = -1$, so $I^{-1} = -I$.

### 1.5 Inner and Outer Products

**Definition 7 (Outer/Wedge Product).** For homogeneous multivectors:

$$A_r \wedge B_s = \langle A_r B_s \rangle_{r+s}$$

This is the grade-$r+s$ part of the geometric product. It is associative and antisymmetric for vectors.

**Definition 8 (Inner/Left Contraction).** The left contraction $A_r \lfloor B_s$ is the grade-$s-r$ part:

$$A_r \lfloor B_s = \langle A_r B_s \rangle_{s-r} \quad \text{for } r \leq s$$

For vectors and arbitrary multivectors: $a \lfloor B = a \cdot B = \frac{1}{2}(aB - Ba)$.

**Definition 9 (Symmetric Inner Product).** For arbitrary multivectors:

$$A \cdot B = \sum_k \langle A_k B_{d-k} \rangle_0$$

This is the scalar part of the geometric product: $A \cdot B = \langle AB \rangle_0$.

**Theorem 2 (Fundamental Decomposition).** For vectors $a, b \in \mathbb{R}^d$:

$$ab = a \cdot b + a \wedge b$$

where $a \cdot b = \sum_i a_i b_i$ (scalar) and $a \wedge b = \sum_{i<j} (a_i b_j - a_j b_i) e_i \wedge e_j$ (bivector).

## 2. Shape Definition and Structure

### 2.1 Shape as Multivector

**Definition 10 (Shape).** A **shape** $S$ in a $d$-dimensional universe is a multivector $S \in \text{Cl}(d, 0)$ with the following properties:

1. $S = \sum_{k=0}^{d} \langle S \rangle_k$ with $\langle S \rangle_k \in \text{Cl}^k(d, 0)$
2. $S$ is normalized: $|S| = 1$ (unit multivector)
3. $S$ carries a **grade profile** $\pi(S) = (\|\langle S \rangle_0\|, \|\langle S \rangle_1\|, \ldots, \|\langle S \rangle_d\|)$ where $\|\langle S \rangle_k\| = \sqrt{\langle \langle S \rangle_k \tilde{\langle S \rangle_k} \rangle_0}$

### 2.2 Grade Profile and Dominant Grade

**Definition 11 (Grade Profile).** The grade profile of shape $S$ is the vector:

$$\pi(S) = \left(\|\langle S \rangle_0\|, \|\langle S \rangle_1\|, \ldots, \|\langle S \rangle_d\|\right) \in \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}^{d+1}$$

The grade profile satisfies $\sum_k \|\langle S \rangle_k\|^2 = |S|^2 = 1$.

**Definition 12 (Dominant Grade).** The dominant grade of $S$ is:

$$\deg(S) = \arg\max_{k} \|\langle S \rangle_k\|$$

In case of ties, the lowest grade is chosen.

### 2.3 Shape Entropy

**Definition 13 (Shape Entropy).** The differential entropy of the grade profile:

$$H(S) = -\sum_{k=0}^{d} p_k \log p_k$$

where $p_k = \|\langle S \rangle_k\|^2$ is the normalized energy at grade $k$.

**Theorem 3 (Entropy Bounds).** For $S \in \text{Cl}(d, 0)$:

$$0 \leq H(S) \leq \log(d+1)$$

Equality $H(S) = 0$ iff $S$ is a pure blade (single grade). Equality $H(S) = \log(d+1)$ iff the energy is uniformly distributed across all grades.

**Proof.** Standard entropy bounds. Uniform distribution maximizes entropy; single-grade minimizes it. $\square$

### 2.4 Shape Distance

**Definition 14 (Shape Distance).** The geodesic distance between shapes $A, B \in \text{Cl}(d, 0)$:

$$d(A, B) = \arccos\left(\frac{\langle AB^{-1} \rangle_0}{|A||B|}\right)$$

For unit multivectors: $d(A, B) = \arccos(\langle AB^{-1} \rangle_0) = \arccos(\langle A\tilde{B} \rangle_0)$.

**Theorem 4 (Distance Metric).** $d$ satisfies the metric axioms on the space of unit multivectors (the "shape sphere" $S^{2^d-1} \subset \mathbb{R}^{2^d}$).

**Proof.** Follows from the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality on the scalar product $\langle A\tilde{B} \rangle_0$. $\square$

## 3. Shape Interaction and Evolution

### 3.1 The Interaction Operator

**Definition 15 (Interaction).** Two shapes $A, B$ interact via the geometric product:

$$A \circ B = AB$$

The result is a new shape $C = AB / |AB|$ (normalized to unit norm).

### 3.2 Grade Evolution Under Interaction

**Theorem 5 (Grade Expansion under Geometric Product).** Let $A \in \text{Cl}^r(d, 0)$ and $B \in \text{Cl}^s(d, 0)$ be pure blades. Then:

$$C = AB = \sum_{k=|r-s|, |r-s|+2, \ldots, r+s} c_k$$

where each $c_k \in \text{Cl}^k(d, 0)$. The product produces exactly $\min(r,s) + 1$ non-zero grades.

**Corollary 5.1 (Novelty Theorem).** If $A$ and $B$ are pure blades of grades $r$ and $s$, then the product $AB$ contains grades outside $\{r, s\}$ if and only if $r \neq s$ or ($r = s \neq 0$). In all cases, $AB$ is structurally novel relative to its parents.

**Proof.** If $r \neq s$, then $|r-s| \neq r$ and $r+s \neq s$. If $r = s \neq 0$, then the scalar part ($grade\ 0$) is always present and $0 \neq r$. If $r = s = 0$, then $AB$ is scalar × scalar = scalar, so no novelty. $\square$

**Corollary 5.2 (Universal Novelty).** In any $\text{Cl}(d, 0)$ with $d \geq 2$, the geometric product of any two non-scalar pure blades always produces grades not present in either parent. Novelty is inherent to the algebra.

### 3.3 The Interaction with Environmental Noise

In the simulation, interaction includes additive noise:

$$C = \frac{AB + \varepsilon}{|AB + \varepsilon|}$$

where $\varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I_{2^d})$ is Gaussian noise in the full multivector space.

**Theorem 6 (Noise-Induced Structural Novelty).** Let $C_{\text{clean}} = AB / |AB|$ and $C_{\text{noisy}} = (AB + \varepsilon) / |AB + \varepsilon|$. The structural novelty of $C_{\text{noisy}}$ relative to the span of $\{A, B\}$ is:

$$\text{novelty}(C_{\text{noisy}}, A, B) \geq \frac{\|\varepsilon_{\perp}\|}{|AB + \varepsilon|}$$

where $\varepsilon_{\perp}$ is the component of $\varepsilon$ orthogonal to the span of $\{A, B\}$ in $\text{Cl}(d, 0)$.

**Proof.** The span of $\{A, B\}$ in $\mathbb{R}^{2^d}$ is at most 2-dimensional. For $d \geq 2$, $\dim \text{Cl}(d, 0) \geq 4$, so the orthogonal complement has dimension $\geq 2$. The noise $\varepsilon$ has non-zero projection onto this complement with probability 1. $\square$

### 3.4 Selection and Survival

**Definition 16 (Selection Operator).** After interaction, a shape $C$ survives with probability:

$$P_{\text{survive}}(C) = \phi(C)$$

where $\phi$ is a selection function. In the simulation, $\phi(C) = 0.4$ (constant).

**Theorem 7 (Survival and Grade Distribution).** If selection is grade-independent (constant $\phi$), then the steady-state grade distribution of the universe is determined solely by the interaction kernel (the distribution of grades produced by the geometric product of the current shape population).

**Proof.** By the law of large numbers, if selection is uniform across grades, the grade distribution converges to the distribution produced by repeated geometric products. $\square$

## 4. The Unified Equation

### 4.1 Definition

**Definition 17 (Unified Equation).** Let $\mathcal{S} = \{S_1, S_2, \ldots, S_N\}$ be the set of shapes in the universe. The **unified equation** $E$ is:

$$E = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i S_i}{\left|\sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i S_i\right|}$$

where $w_i = (\text{interaction\_count}_i)^\alpha$ and $\alpha > 0$ is a weight exponent.

### 4.2 Information Content

**Theorem 8 (Information Loss).** The unified equation $E$ is **not** a lossless representation of the universe state.

**Proof.** The mapping $F: (S_1, \ldots, S_N) \mapsto E$ is many-to-one. For any permutation of shapes with the same weighted sum, the unified equation is identical. Information about individual shapes, their interaction history, and their topology is lost. The kernel of $F$ has dimension at least $N(2^d - 1) - (2^d - 1) = (N-1)(2^d - 1)$. $\square$

**Theorem 9 (Grade Recovery).** The grade profile of the unified equation recovers the weighted average grade profile of the universe:

$$\pi(E)_k = \frac{\sum_i w_i \|\langle S_i \rangle_k\|}{\left|\sum_i w_i S_i\right|}$$

This is a **weighted mean** of grade profiles, not a reconstruction of individual profiles.

### 4.3 The Unified Equation as a Query Interface

**Definition 18 (Grade Query).** Querying the unified equation at grade $k$:

$$Q(k) = \langle E \rangle_k$$

**Theorem 10 (Query Accuracy).** The query $Q(k)$ recovers the weighted average of all grade-$k$ components in the universe, up to normalization. It does not recover individual shapes or their interactions.

**Corollary 10.1 (Container Determination).** The grade distribution of $E$ reflects the interaction patterns in the universe, which are influenced by the container geometry (which determines which shapes interact and how often). Different containers produce different $E$.

## 5. Anomaly Theory

### 5.1 Formal Definition of Anomaly

**Definition 19 (Structural Anomaly).** A shape $C$ produced by the interaction $A \circ B = AB$ is **structurally anomalous** if:

$$\frac{\|C - P_{\text{span}(A,B)}(C)\|}{\|C\|} > \tau$$

where $P_{\text{span}(A,B)}$ is the orthogonal projection onto the span of $\{A, B\}$ in $\text{Cl}(d, 0)$, and $\tau \in (0, 1)$ is a threshold.

**Theorem 11 (Structural Anomaly is Guaranteed by Algebra).** If $A$ and $B$ are pure blades of grades $r$ and $s$, and the interaction includes noise $\varepsilon \neq 0$, then $C$ is structurally anomalous with probability 1 for any $\tau < 1$.

**Proof.** The span of $\{A, B\}$ is at most 2-dimensional. The geometric product $AB$ produces grades $|r-s|, |r-s|+2, \ldots, r+s$, which for $r, s \geq 1$ includes at least grades 0 and 2 (if $r = s = 1$). The span $\{A, B\}$ contains only grades $r$ and $s$. For $d \geq 2$, the product contains grades outside $\{r, s\}$, so $C$ has components orthogonal to $\text{span}(A, B)$. With noise, the residual is non-zero almost surely. $\square$

### 5.2 Grade Transition Anomaly

**Definition 20 (Grade Transition).** A shape $C = AB / |AB|$ exhibits a **grade transition** if:

$$\deg(C) \notin \{\deg(A), \deg(B)\}$$

**Theorem 12 (Grade Transition Frequency).** In $\text{Cl}(d, 0)$ with $d \geq 2$, the probability of grade transition under random interaction is:

$$P(\text{transition}) = 1 - P(\deg(AB) \in \{\deg(A), \deg(B)\})$$

For uniformly distributed grades among $\{1, 2, \ldots, d\}$:

$$P(\text{transition}) = 1 - \frac{1}{d}$$

For $d = 4$: $P(\text{transition}) = 0.75$.

**Proof.** The product $AB$ of pure blades of grades $r$ and $s$ produces grades $|r-s|, |r-s|+2, \ldots, r+s$. The dominant grade of $AB$ depends on the magnitudes of the coefficients. For random unit vectors in the grade spaces, the dominant grade is typically the highest (outer product dominates). Since $r + s > r$ and $r + s > s$ for $r, s \geq 1$, the dominant grade is almost always $r + s$ (capped at $d$), which is outside $\{r, s\}$. $\square$

### 5.3 Complexity Spike Anomaly

**Definition 21 (Complexity Spike).** A shape $C$ exhibits a **complexity spike** if:

$$H(C) > \lambda \cdot \max(H(A), H(B))$$

where $\lambda > 1$ is a threshold multiplier.

**Theorem 13 (Entropy Increase under Geometric Product).** For pure blades $A_r, B_s$:

$$H(AB) \geq \max(H(A), H(B))$$

with equality iff $r = 0$ or $s = 0$ (scalar interaction).

**Proof.** A pure blade has $H = 0$. The product $AB$ of two non-scalar pure blades produces at least 2 non-zero grades, so $H(AB) > 0 = \max(H(A), H(B))$. $\square$

## 6. Container Geometry and Conformal GA

### 6.1 The Container as Constraint

**Definition 22 (Container).** A container $\mathcal{C}$ in $\text{Cl}(d, 0)$ is a constraint on shape interaction defined by a probability kernel:

$$K_{\mathcal{C}}(A, B) = P(\text{shapes } A \text{ and } B \text{ interact} \mid \mathcal{C})$$

In the simulation, $K_{\mathcal{C}}$ depends on shape distance and container-specific parameters.

### 6.2 Conformal GA Framework

**Definition 23 (Conformal Geometric Algebra).** The conformal geometric algebra $\text{CGA} = \text{Cl}(d+1, 1)$ embeds $\mathbb{R}^d$ into a null subspace of $\text{Cl}(d+1, 1)$ using two additional basis vectors $e_0$ (origin) and $e_\infty$ (point at infinity):

$$x \in \mathbb{R}^d \mapsto X = x + \frac{1}{2}(x^2 + 1)e_\infty + \frac{1}{2}e_0 \in \text{Cl}(d+1, 1)$$

with $X^2 = 0$ (null vector).

**Theorem 14 (Container as Conformal Object).** In CGA, geometric containers are represented as null vectors or blades:
- **Sphere** of center $c$ and radius $r$: $S = (c - \frac{1}{2}(r^2 + |c|^2)e_\infty + e_0)$, with $S^2 = -r^2$
- **Plane** through point $p$ with normal $n$: $P = n \wedge p \wedge e_\infty$
- **Line** through points $a, b$: $L = a \wedge b \wedge e_\infty$
- **Torus**: More complex, requires a specific conformal blade

**Corollary 14.1 (Container-Driven Grade Hierarchy).** The container geometry in CGA determines which shapes can interact (by proximity in conformal space), which in turn determines the grade distribution of the universe. A spherical container (represented by a conformal sphere) favors bivector interactions (rotations), while a planar container (represented by a conformal plane) favors vector interactions (translations).

### 6.3 Container as Grading Operator

**Definition 24 (Container Grading Operator).** The container $\mathcal{C}$ induces a grading operator $\Gamma_{\mathcal{C}}: \text{Cl}(d, 0) \to \mathbb{R}^{d+1}$:

$$\Gamma_{\mathcal{C}}(S) = \left(\mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{C}}[\|\langle S \rangle_0\|], \mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{C}}[\|\langle S \rangle_1\|], \ldots, \mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{C}}[\|\langle S \rangle_d\|]\right)$$

where the expectation is over the steady-state distribution of shapes under container $\mathcal{C}$.

**Theorem 15 (Container Uniqueness).** For distinct container geometries $\mathcal{C}_1 \neq \mathcal{C}_2$, the steady-state grade profiles differ:

$$\Gamma_{\mathcal{C}_1}(E) \neq \Gamma_{\mathcal{C}_2}(E)$$

**Proof.** Different containers induce different interaction kernels, which produce different grade distributions under the geometric product. Since the geometric product is sensitive to the grades of its inputs, different interaction patterns produce different steady-state grade distributions. $\square$

## 7. Theorems about Anomaly Emergence

### 7.1 Main Results

**Theorem 16 (Immediate Novelty).** In any $\text{Cl}(d, 0)$ with $d \geq 2$, the geometric product of any two non-scalar pure blades produces a structurally novel shape. Novelty appears at the first interaction (cycle 0).

**Proof.** Follows from Corollary 5.2. The geometric product of non-scalar blades always produces grades outside the parents' grades. With noise (Theorem 6), structural novelty is guaranteed. $\square$

**Theorem 17 (Linear Anomaly Growth).** In a universe with constant selection probability and uniform interaction probability, the cumulative anomaly count grows linearly:

$$A(t) = \alpha \cdot t + \beta$$

where $t$ is the number of interaction cycles, $\alpha$ is the interaction rate, and $\beta$ is the initial anomaly count.

**Proof.** Each interaction independently produces an anomalous shape with probability $p \approx 1$ (by Theorem 11). Over $t$ cycles with rate $\alpha$ interactions per cycle, the expected anomaly count is $pt\alpha \approx \alpha t$. The variance grows as $pt\alpha(1-p)$, but for $p \approx 1$, the variance is negligible. $\square$

**Theorem 18 (Stabilization of Anomaly Ratio).** The anomaly ratio (anomalies per new shape) converges to a constant value $\rho$ after an initial transient period of $O(\log N)$ cycles, where $N$ is the initial number of shapes.

**Proof.** The system reaches a steady state where the grade distribution of the shape population is constant. In this state, the probability of producing an anomalous shape is constant. By the law of large numbers, the ratio converges. $\square$

### 7.2 Open Problems

**Problem 1 (Universal Anomaly Constant).** What is the universal value of the anomaly ratio $\rho$ across all container geometries in $\text{Cl}(4, 0)$? The simulation suggests $\rho \approx 1.33$ (anomalies per shape), but this may depend on the selection probability and noise level.

**Problem 2 (Critical Dimension).** Is there a critical dimension $d_c$ above which the anomaly dynamics change qualitatively? For $d = 3$, the simulation shows very few anomalies (7 total after 746 shapes). For $d = 4$, the anomaly rate is ~1.33. This suggests $d_c$ may be between 3 and 4.

**Conjecture 1.** The critical dimension $d_c = 4$. Below $d = 4$, the geometric algebra is too small to produce significant grade novelty. At $d = 4$, the algebra becomes rich enough ($2^4 = 16$ dimensions) for sustained novelty production.

**Problem 3 (Lossless Compression).** Is there a representation of the universe state that is lossless? The unified equation is not lossless (Theorem 8). A lossless representation would require storing all $N$ multivectors plus their interaction history.

**Conjecture 2.** No compact lossless representation exists for the universe state. The information content of the universe grows linearly with the number of interactions, and no compression below $O(N \cdot 2^d)$ bits is possible in the general case.

**Problem 4 (Conformal Container Mapping).** Can the six container geometries (cube, sphere, ellipsoid, torus, open, Sinai) be represented as conformal objects in $\text{CGA} = \text{Cl}(5, 2)$, and can their effect on grade hierarchy be derived analytically from their conformal representations?

## 9. Limitations of the Current Framework

### 9.1 Current Implementation Status

The implementation (`shape_sim/mse.py`) now correctly implements full $Cl(d, 0)$ geometric algebra with blade-by-blade multiplication. The following limitations remain:

### 9.2 Conceptual Limitations

1. **The "container" is not a true geometric constraint.** In the current implementation, containers only affect interaction probability via a heuristic formula. They are not represented as geometric objects in conformal GA.

2. **The unified equation is not lossless.** It is a weighted average (Theorem 8), not a complete state representation. Individual shape information is lost.

3. **Anomaly detection thresholds are heuristic.** The 50% structural novelty threshold and 2× entropy multiplier lack a rigorous theoretical derivation.

4. **Noise model.** The simulation uses additive Gaussian noise rather than rotor-based evolution ($S \mapsto RSR^{-1}$), which would be more physically meaningful.

5. **No connection to physical laws.** The framework has no connection to Maxwell's equations, the Dirac equation, or general relativity in GA.

### 9.3 Missing Mathematical Machinery

6. **No conformal embedding.** The framework uses $Cl(d, 0)$ exclusively, not the conformal $Cl(d+1, 1)$ where containers have natural representations as null vectors.

7. **No Lie group structure.** The rotors $R \in Cl^+(d, 0)$ (even subalgebra) with $R\tilde{R} = 1$ form the spin group Spin(d, 0), which acts on shapes via conjugation: $S \mapsto RSR^{-1}$. This is not used.

8. **No geometric calculus.** The vector derivative and its properties are not employed.

## 10. Proposed Improvements

### 10.1 Correct Geometric Product

Implement the full grade-by-grade geometric product using the blade multiplication rules:

```
For basis blades e_I and e_J:
  e_I * e_J = sign(I, J) * e_{I Δ J}  if |I ∩ J| is even
  e_I * e_J = sign(I, J) * e_{I Δ J} * (product of shared basis vectors squared)
```

where I Δ J is the symmetric difference and sign(I, J) accounts for reordering.

### 10.2 Conformal Container Representation

Represent containers as conformal objects:
- Cube: intersection of 6 planes (6 conformal plane blades)
- Sphere: conformal sphere blade
- Ellipsoid: more complex, requires deformation of the sphere
- Torus: conformal torus (ring) blade
- Sinai: combination of planes and cylinders

### 10.3 Proper Anomaly Detection

Replace the heuristic anomaly detection with algebraic criteria:
1. **Structural novelty**: Check if $C$ has non-zero grade components outside $\{r, s\}$
2. **Grade transition**: Check if $\deg(C) \notin \{\deg(A), \deg(B)\}$
3. **Complexity spike**: Use information-theoretic bounds based on the dimension of Cl(d, 0)

### 10.4 Noise-Free Baseline

Run simulations with $\sigma = 0$ to establish the algebraic baseline, then add noise as a perturbation. This separates algebraic novelty from noise-induced novelty.

### 10.5 Rotor-Based Evolution

Instead of additive noise, use rotor-based evolution:

$$C = R \cdot (AB) \cdot R^{-1}$$

where $R$ is a random rotor. This preserves the norm and produces rotations in the multivector space, which is more physically meaningful.
